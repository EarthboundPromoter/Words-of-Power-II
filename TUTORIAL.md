# Words of Power — a tutorial for new players

Welcome. This guide is in two parts: first the game, then the mod that speaks it. If you've never played a game like Rift Wizard, start at the top. If you know the game and just want the mod, jump to Part 2. Nothing here is required reading — F1 in game gives you the mod's full reference — but this is the tour I'd give you if we were sitting together.

## Part 1: Rift Wizard 3, the game

### What kind of game this is

Rift Wizard 3 is a tactical roguelike. You are a wizard descending through 21 rifts — self-contained battle levels — to reach and destroy Mordred. The object of every rift is total: kill every entity on it, spawners included. When the last one falls, the level chime sounds — and pressing A from there auto-walks you to every pickup you haven't collected yet. Every rift is procedurally generated, so no two runs are alike. Death is permanent: when you die, the run is over and the next one starts fresh. That sounds harsh, but it's the engine of the whole game — every run teaches you something the next one uses.

The game is turn-based on a grid. Nothing moves until you do. Take a step, cast a spell, or pass, and then every enemy takes its turn. There is no time pressure, ever. You can sit on a single turn for ten minutes, check everything twice, and the game will wait. Good players do exactly that on hard turns.

### Your spells

Spells are your entire offense and most of your defense. You buy them with SP — skill points — at the spell shop, opened with S, anytime. SP comes mainly from Memory Orbs, small pickups scattered through the rifts that grant 1 SP each when you step on them.

The spell list is enormous, and that's normal — experimentation is a core tenet of the game, so don't be frightened by the size of it. Think of it as a deadly candy store.

Every spell has charges: a limited number of casts. When you leave a rift, you arrive at the next one fully refreshed — all health, all charges — so charges are a per-level budget, not a lifetime one. Spending your best spell freely in a hard fight is usually right; hoarding it until the level is over is usually wrong.

Each spell can also hold two upgrades, bought with SP from the spell's own upgrade menu. This sets up the recurring decision of the whole game: spend SP on upgrades to make what you have stronger, or on new spells to widen your options. Depth versus breadth. There's no universal answer — but know that upgrades can vastly change how a spell behaves, not just make it bigger. And because each spell holds two, you're really choosing a pairing: the right combination is often a different weapon entirely. A build with no second option can be shut out cold (more on that under rifts).

### Equipment, components, and crafting

You don't buy equipment — you craft it from components. Components are pickups; each one carries one or more tags, like Fire or Nature or Metallic. Picking one up does two things: its tags go into your crafting bank, and most components also fire an immediate effect — a burst of shields, a heal, a temporary aura. Grab them even when you don't need the tags; the pickup effects are real resources and they're best used when the timing helps you.

Crafting turns banked tags into equipment: passive items that shape your build the way skill trees do in other games. Two rules worth knowing, because they confuse everyone:

You can only ever own one copy of each named item. Craft the Staff of Whatever once, and it's gone from your options.

Slots — weapon, helmet, armor, boots, trinket — are just categories. They don't limit anything. You can wear three helmets. The one-copy rule is the only restriction, and the Duplication Shrine is its only exception.

One more layer: rare components. Instead of a pickup effect, a rare fires when you craft with it, permanently building its bonus into the finished item — extra damage, extra charges, a new triggered effect. Rares reward matching: a rare that boosts Translocation gear does nothing crafted into a fire staff. When you're holding one, it's worth planning the craft around it.

### Landmarks

Rifts contain more than enemies. The things worth walking to:

Menu landmarks offer a choice: step on one, and you pick a single option from a short list. Nothing is spent — the pick itself is the whole transaction. Some offer spells, equipment, or blessings. The shrines each do one strange, powerful thing — the Shrine of Reformation lets you permanently remove a spell or item and get its cost back; the Shrine of Knowledge trades one of your components for a random spell sharing its tags; others duplicate, evolve, or dissolve what you own. Read what's offered; the option to walk away is always free.

Walk-on landmarks fire the moment you step on them. Ruby Hearts raise your maximum health by 25 and heal you to full. Memory Orbs grant the SP mentioned above. A few rare shrines transform you outright — read them with the examine key before stepping on if you like surprises to be consensual.

### Enemies and spawners

Every enemy type has its own behavior, and the differences are the game. Some walk at you. Some hang back and shoot. Some fly over chasms your other threats can't cross, some teleport, some explode when they die, some heal or shield the others. When you meet something new, examine it — its description tells you what it does before it does it.

Spawners deserve their own mention: they're structures that produce new enemies every few turns, indefinitely. A level with active spawners gets harder the longer you take. Finding them and deciding when to silence them is a core part of reading a level.

### Damage, resistances, and shields

Every attack has a damage type, and every creature has resistances to some types. Resistance is a percentage: 50 fire resistance means fire hits for half. 100 means immune. And it goes negative — a creature with minus 100 fire resistance takes double fire damage. Vulnerabilities are how modest spells kill big things; immunities are how big spells do nothing. This applies to you too — your own resistances work the same way.

Shields are the other defensive layer: each shield blocks one entire hit, whatever its size, then breaks. They stack up to 20. A single shield is worthless against a swarm of rats and priceless against a dragon.

### Rifts, and choosing your next one

When a rift is cleared, portals open to the next set. Before entering a portal, you can examine it and see what lives on the other side — the enemies, the landmarks, the rewards. This is a real decision, because hard counters exist: if your build is all fire and the rift ahead is packed with fire-immune enemies, that rift is close to unwinnable for you, and the game will not stop you from walking in. Check the portals. Pick fights your build can win. You also have a limited number of rift rerolls that replace the current portals with fresh ones — for when every door is a bad door.

### Deploying

Here's the step players most often waste: when you enter a rift, you don't appear at a fixed spot. You see the whole level first and choose your starting tile — almost anywhere walkable. This is called deploying, and it may be the single most leveraged decision you make per level.

Deploying is a two-stage process, and the stages carry very different weights. Stage one is pressing Enter on a portal: that's a peek, not a commitment. The level lays itself out and the cursor is yours — look around as long as you like. The only thing that commits you is stage two: pressing Enter on a tile. Until then you can back out entirely, and peeking into several rifts before choosing is a genuinely good habit when you're unsure where to go — geography is a factor beyond enemy composition, and two rifts with the same monsters can play completely differently depending on the ground.

You arrive refreshed, but you arrive alone, and everything on the map already knows where you are. Deploy in the middle of open ground and everything converges on you at once. Deploy into a corner or behind a chokepoint and enemies come to you a few at a time. Deploy near a spawner and you can put it down before it earns its keep; deploy far from all of them and you fight reinforcements all level. A bad deploy can lose the run on turn one. A good one turns a frightening level into a queue.

Take your time on the deploy screen. The mod gives you tools built for exactly this — see Part 2.

## Part 2: The mod — Words of Power

The mod speaks what a sighted player sees: the map, the menus, the log, the tooltips. It never plays for you and never tells you what it can't see. What follows is the mental model and the core verbs; the complete key list lives on F1.

### Looking and acting

The one distinction that organizes everything: at any moment you are either acting — moving your wizard, casting — or looking. Press V and a cursor appears; that's look mode. Move the cursor around, and each tile announces what's on it. Escape puts the cursor away and you're back to acting. Nothing you do in look mode touches the game — looking is always free.

### Scans: your eyes

Scans are faster than walking a cursor around. Press I and the mod speaks the nearest enemy — name, direction, distance. Press I again for the next nearest, and again, cycling outward. U scans allies the same way, O scans landmarks, N scans spawners. Shift reverses any cycle.

Scans are pure information: they move nothing, not even the cursor. Think of scans as your eyes and the cursor as your hand. Your eyes can sweep the whole room while your hand stays exactly where you put it.

The bridge between them is J: jump. J moves the cursor to the last thing a scan spoke. Scan until you hear the one you care about, press J, and your hand is now on it. Shift+J puts the cursor back where it was. This eyes-then-hand rhythm — scan, scan, scan, J — is the fastest way to get anywhere, and it works everywhere a cursor exists.

### Casting a spell

Press a number key, 1 through 0, to select that spell. An aim appears, starting on your own tile — so if you cast immediately, you target yourself. Move the aim with the arrows, or press Tab to snap it between targets the spell can actually hit, or scan and press J to put the aim on something the scan found. Enter casts. Escape backs out, spell unspent.

While you're aiming, scans keep working, and they measure from your aim rather than from you — "From aim" — so you can check what's around the spot you're about to hit. If the cast fails, the mod tells you why: out of range, no line of sight, not enough charges.

### Paths: how far is that?

P speaks the walking route to your attention point: to the cursor in look mode, to the aim while casting, and with no cursor up, to the last thing a scan spoke. The route comes compressed — "east 3, north 2, arrive" — and it's the honest path, around walls and chasms, not the crow's distance. Scan an enemy, press P, and you know how many turns it needs to reach you or you it. Shift+P gives the route to your focused pin.

### What speaks on its own

You don't have to ask for everything. The mod announces the events a sighted player can't miss: damage dealt and taken, deaths, things entering your line of sight, enemies arriving next to you, your turn starting. If the mod is quiet, the level is quiet. Trust the silence; it's load-bearing.

### Pins: your working memory

Alt+K pins the last thing a scan spoke (or the tile you're looking at). Pins persist. The newest pin is your focused pin, and it speaks a step toward itself each turn; every pin announces its own death or disappearance. K cycles through all of them in category blocks.

Pins are the answer to "how do I hold a plan in my head across forty turns?" Some uses that earn their keep: pin every spawner during deploy and work down the list. Pin a Ruby Heart to come back for once the room between you is empty. Pin the melee bruiser that's hunting you, so its each-turn step tells you how close it is. Pin the tile you're retreating toward. You get to be systematic about a level instead of re-scanning it every turn.

### Line of sight, and why it runs the game

Most spells require line of sight: an unbroken straight path between you and the target. Walls block it both ways — which makes LoS a resource you manage, not a fact you suffer. Stand where the archers can't see you and only the melee line matters; step out, cast, step back.

L speaks the enemies in your line of sight by type and direction. T tells you whether your tile is threatened — whether something can hit you where you stand. Both work from the cursor too, so you can evaluate a tile before committing to it: look there, press T, and know if it's safe before you take the step. When a scan says "blocked," that enemy exists but can't be hit from here — and usually can't hit you either.

### Area spells

When you aim a spell with an area — a fireball's blast radius, the chaining targets of mass freeze, the beam of a lightning bolt — the mod reads the spell's real footprint and tells you who's standing in it, allies included. You'll hear when you're in your own blast. The workflow that follows: Tab or J onto the target cluster, listen to the census, then nudge the aim a tile or two until the footprint holds everything you want and nothing you don't. The footprint is computed by the game itself, whatever the spell's shape — splash, beam, chain, or stranger — so what you hear is what will burn.

### Getting around

Movement is the standard eight directions. The idiom worth knowing: press two arrows together for a diagonal.

Looking has its own gears, and two of them deserve special billing, because together they're the fastest way to see anywhere on the map without leaving your tile. Hold Ctrl and press a direction to peek: a temporary look cursor appears at your feet and steps wherever you point, for as long as Ctrl is held — release, and it vanishes, nothing spent. Ctrl+Shift+direction is the jump: it skims the cursor along a line straight to the next interesting thing — a unit, an item, a wall — announcing what it crossed on the way. And once a look cursor is up, Shift with a direction strides it four tiles at a time — the middle gear between stepping and jumping. Peek to feel out the ground around you; jump to case a whole corridor in one press. "Edge" means the map ends that way.

One asymmetry to know: the numpad works for plain steps and diagonals, but Windows interferes with the Ctrl+Shift numpad chords, so jumps live on the arrow keys. Everything the mod does is reachable from the letters and arrows; the numpad is a convenience, not a requirement.

### Deploying with the mod

The deploy screen gets its own toolkit, because it's the level's most important decision made all at once. Press 1 for an overview of the incoming level. 2 cycles the Memory Orbs, 3 the pickups, 4 the spawners, 5 the shops. All the normal scans work too, measured from your deploy cursor, and pins you set during deploy carry into the level.

A deploy routine that works: 1 for the shape of things, 4 to place every spawner (pin them), a pass with I to hear where the enemies mass, then walk the cursor to the quiet corner of the map and confirm. Thirty seconds of listening buys you the whole level.

### The shop screen

Stepping on a shop or opening the spell shop puts you in a menu: arrow through the options, and each speaks its name, cost, and description. Enter buys the selected item; Escape leaves without buying. The catalog screens have filters — Tab speaks the filter guide — and a search box for finding a specific option by name. When the search box has focus it swallows normal keys, so the mod tells you when you're in it and out of it.

### The crafting screen

Same shape as the shop: a list of every piece of equipment, arrow through, Enter to craft the selected one. Each entry tells you its recipe — which tags it needs — and whether you can afford it from your bank. Craftable items are what you're browsing for, but the uncraftable entries are worth a listen too: they're your shopping list for which components to grab next level.

### The character sheet

C opens your character sheet: your spells with their upgrades, your equipment, and your component tag bank — the crafting currency you're carrying, spoken by tag. Arrow through the lists. Enter on a spell opens its upgrade purchases, so upgrading lives here as well as in the shops. Hold the modifier key with up and down to reorder the selected spell or item in its list — spell order is which number key casts what, so put your everyday spells on 1 and 2.

### Tooltips go deep

Descriptions in this game nest. A piece of equipment that grants a spell carries that spell's full description; a spell that applies a curse carries the curse's. When you examine something, the mod reads the whole stack, because the bottom of the stack is often where the real answer lives. D speaks the full detail of whatever is under the cursor — tile, unit, or menu entry. When a fight surprises you, the explanation is almost always in a description you haven't read yet.

### Controlling the speech

Ctrl silences the current speech instantly. Z repeats the last thing said. Left and right brackets walk back and forward through everything the mod has said this session. You will use Ctrl constantly, and that's by design — interrupt freely, the information isn't going anywhere.

### Where to go from here

F1 opens the mod's full help, ending with a tips page for new players. Shift+slash speaks a quick keybind reference without leaving the game. The game's own help is on H, with the mod's guide appended. The mod's settings live in settings.ini next to the mod, with comments explaining each option — coordinates on or off, verbosity choices, and more.

That's the tour. Deploy carefully, read the descriptions, respect the spawners, and remember the game will wait as long as you need. Good hunting.
